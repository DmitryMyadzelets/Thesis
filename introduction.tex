\section{Introducton to Introduction}

Modern society is characterized by the growing demand for the usage of automated
systems in the everyday life, with constantly increasing complexity of software
engineering. Industrial automation software engineering traditionally was a
field which has the most strict requirements and high standards applied, which
is also reflected in its high cost. Reflecting the importance of the software
and a growing ratio of the software cost in the costs of machinery, engineers
and researches put a lot of effort to facilitate the development and maintenance
of software, increasing its performance and reliability, while preserving or 
decreasing the cost of its life cycle.

\section{Context}

According to the standard ISO/IEC 12207 \cite{_iso/iec_12207_2008} the
industrial software development process (life cycle process) can be structured
into six stages: requirements specification, software design, implementation and
integration, testing, deployment and maintenance. The implementation and
the following stages may depend on a particular hardware manufacture, but the
variety of approaches the developers can use is limited by the standards, such
as {IEC 61131}, making it less error prone and more reusable. The stage of
requirements specification consists of analyzing, documenting and validating the
needs and conditions for technological process, as well as rules, constrains and
policies for the plant hardware. The most known standard used at this stage
is the Unified Modeling Language (UML), developed by the Object Management
Group (OMG) Technology Standards Consortium. This standard facilitates also  
the next stage, software design, as a Model-Driven Architecture (MDA) approach.
The industrial software design stage adopts also other numerous approaches,
such as component based approach, object-oriented and aspect-oriented programming,
software product line and etc. A survey of the state of the art for software
engineering methods in industrial automation can be found in
\cite{vyatkin_software_2013}.

Even if some of the aforementioned approaches try to cover all the stages of
industrial software development process, their drawback is that they are mainly
designed for humans, whereas the reliability of systems is mainly a concern of
machine-oriented formal verification methods. Formal methods apply
mathematically-based techniques to the development of systems, starting from the
specification level, and down to the implementation level. These methods proved
to be effective for safety critical systems, but due to mathematical
nature of these methods and lack of the tool support their use in industrial
practice is not yet common. Approaches which exploit formal methods sometimes
suffer from limitations which are inseparable from these methods, like the state
explosion problem.

\section{Restatement of the problem}

Nowadays, when the growing complexity of automated systems imposes requirements
which can be ensured only by formal methods, the researches and engineers
take two distinguished ways for their approaches. One is mixing of formal
techniques of ``standard-based'' approaches/tools that are already
adopted in industry. Despite some successful reports can be found in
literature, the progress seems low and applicable upon numerous constrains
for the methods and tools. Another way is a development of new approaches
which incorporate advantages of both, suitable for humans methodologies, and
formal techniques.
