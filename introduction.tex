
\chapter{Introduction}


% \section{Introducton to Introduction}

Modern society is characterized by the growing demand for the usage of automated
systems in the everyday life, with constantly increasing complexity of software
engineering. Industrial automation software engineering traditionally was a
field which has the most strict requirements and high standards applied.
However, it still common the practice when a software design in automation
consists of writing a ladder logic for programmable logic controller (PLC), with
partly and ambiguously defined specifications, with no clearly defined software
architecture, and neither validation nor formal verification of the system.
Reflecting the importance of the software and a growing ratio of the software
cost in the costs of machinery, engineers and researches put a lot of effort to
facilitate the development and maintenance of software, increasing its
performance and reliability while preserving or decreasing the cost of its life
cycle.

% \section{Context}

According to the standard ISO/IEC 12207 \cite{_iso/iec_12207_2008} the
industrial software development process (life cycle process) can be structured
into six stages: requirements specification, software design, implementation and
integration, testing, deployment and maintenance. The implementation and the
following stages may depend on a particular hardware manufacture. There are
hundreds major producers of automation hardware, but the variety of approaches
the developers can use is limited by the standards, such as {IEC 61131}, making
it less error prone and more reusable. The stage of requirements specification
consists of analyzing, documenting and validating the needs and conditions for
technological process, as well as rules, constrains and policies for the plant
hardware. The most known standard used at this stage is the Unified Modeling
Language (UML), developed by the Object Management Group (OMG) Technology
Standards Consortium. This standard facilitates also the next stage, software
design, with a Model-Driven Architecture (MDA) approach.
The industrial software design stage adopts also other numerous techniques, such
as component based approach, object-oriented and aspect-oriented programming,
software product line and etc. An extensive survey of the state of the art for
software engineering methods in industrial automation is presented in
\cite{vyatkin_software_2013}.

Even if some of the aforementioned approaches pretend to cover most of the
stages of industrial software development process, and become a 'de facto'
industrial standard, it would be hardly possible. Their major drawback is that
they are mainly designed for humans, whereas the reliability of systems is
mainly a concern of machine-oriented formal verification methods.
Formal methods apply mathematically-based techniques to the development of
systems, starting from the specification level, and down to the implementation
level. These methods proved to be effective for safety critical systems, but due
to mathematical nature of these methods and lack of the tools support their use
in industrial practice is not yet common. Additionally, approaches which exploit
formal methods sometimes suffer from limitations which are inseparable from
these methods, like the state explosion problem.

% \section{Restatement of the problem}

Nowadays, when the growing complexity of automated systems imposes requirements
which can be ensured only by formal methods, the researches and engineers
take two distinguished ways for their approaches. One is mix of formal
techniques with ``standard-based'' approaches/tools that are already
adopted in industry. Despite the fact that some successful reports can be found
in literature, the progress seems low and applicable only with numerous
constrains both for the methods and tools. Another way is a development of new
approaches which incorporate advantages of both, suitable for humans
methodologies, and formal techniques.

As soon as the quantity and quality of mathematical methods and their
implementations will be enough for the current complexity of automated systems,
and these methods will be encapsulated into integrated development tools as a
first-class citizen, seamlessly providing the power of formal approaches through
the easy-to-understand visual modeling process, the development process of
automated systems will move to the next stage of its evolution. 

% \section{Restatement of the response}

The aim of this work is, in general, to facilitate the process of industrial
automated systems development, applying formal methods to ensure the reliability
of a system. Among many existing problems this work considers the problem of
verification of system's design in terms of diagnosability. Diagnosability
problem answers the question: either the given system is ready for fault
diagnosis or not. According to IEC vocabulary \cite{iec_vocabulary} the
\emph{fault diagnosis} are ``actions taken for fault recognition, fault
localization and cause identification'', and a \emph{fault} is ``the state of an
item characterized by inability to perform a required function''.

This work exploits Discrete Event Systems theory
\cite{cassandras_introduction_2010} and the automata framework. The theory of
automata is chosen due to its relative simplicity. Indeed, it has just two basic
entities (state and event) and intuitively understandable graphical
representation. Taking apart a language theory, which is necessary for
researches, the automata framework does not require much additional mathematical
knowledge for automation engineers. This makes it the first candidate as a
formal mathematical tool for future general purpose integrated development
environments (IDE) in automation industry.

As a way to tackle the state explosion problem which arises when the automata
framework is applied in complex discrete event systems, this work presents a new
formulation of distributed diagnosability problem. This new formulation
is used then to enforce the desired property of the system, rather then just
verifying it.

This work also presents structural patterns in terms of automata
framework. These patterns aimed to decrease computation burden while applying
verification algorithms.

At the higher level of the systems' representation a modification of
``standard'' modular design approach is used, suitable for UML design tools.
Particularly, this work extends an approach of \emph{Generalized Device},
recently developed in the University of Bologna
\cite{faldella_hierarchical_2008}, \cite{sartini_architectures_2010}.

To validate the concepts developed during the research, a special tool was
created. This tool exploits web-technologies in order to provide zero-time
access to it, possibility for easy modifications for other problems, and
opportunity for further enhancement by community.

The next chapter gives the overview of the problem of fault diagnosis on a real
example of the industrial automation plant. Chapter three describes the automata
framework for discrete event systems, modeling approach and how it is used for
diagnosability problem. Chapter four presents a new type of diagnosability -
modular virtual diagnosability, and its relation to existing types of
diagnosability. Chapter five introduces a new tool for modeling and simulation.
